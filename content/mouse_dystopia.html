Universe 25 aka mouse dystopia

tags: science, psychology

Sources

Mouse Heaven or Mouse Hell? | Science History Institute TIL there was a man-made mouse utopia called Universe 25 Behavioral sink - Wikipedia Sci-Hub | The original publication in Scientific American

Notes

  • John Calhoun built the colony at the National Institute of Mental Health in Maryland in 1968
    • he grew up in Tennessee
    • he was tasked to determine what caused rodents (e.g. mice) overpopulate in cities
      • this led to him setting up experiments with large rodent population
      • he also saw potential in extrapolating perfect rodent cities to perfect human cities
  • it was a small 1,3x1,3x1,3m cube where the mice had everything they needed for survival
    • plenty of food and water
    • a perfect climate
    • reams of paper to make cozy nests
    • 256 separate apartments
    • mice were also screened for any disease
  • theoretically mice could live here to long age without any hinderences (i.e. the definition of heaven)
  • this was his most famous experment - 25th of the many

Timeline

  • month 0 - first mice are planted into the cube
  • month 3.5 - first pups are born
    • → population doubles every 55 days
    • growth started stagnating
  • month 19 - population peaks at 2 200 mice
  • month 21 - population starts to plummet and births stop
  • month 58 - utopia goes extinct

Outcomes

  • wild infant mortality among mice is high contrasting with the remarkably low mortality in the utopia
    • there were many more young mice than would be usual
  • in rodent society some male mice would natually lose their alpha position in a fight
    • in nature that would result in leaving for elsewhere
    • in utopian crowded place there was nowhere to leave for
  • → those two factors combined led to huge numbers of "dropouts" who would regularily engage in huge fights
    • those fights generally served no purpose as opposed to the ones forming the social hierarchy
    • occasionally dropouts turned into cannibalism
  • alpha males struggled as well because they had to constantly defend their position (and their living quarters)
    • they grew exhausted and sometimes just quit defending
    • as a result nursing females (who would have otherwise been defended by the alphas) were regularily attacked together with their pups
    • many pups left their mothers in fear way too early as a consequence
    • sometimes mothers left the pups in a run for their own lifes away from the violence
      • → pups died of neglect
  • mice who had troublesome childhood (see above) developed other deviant behaviour
    • weak social bonds (due to social problems in early age)
    • maladjusted females began isolating themselves like hermits in empty apartments
      • unusual behaviour for mice
    • maladjusted males took to grooming all day
      • preening and licking themselves hour after hour
      • oddly enough they had no interest in opposite gender and sexual behaviour despite being "beautiful"
    • it was noticed that this maladjusted behaviour can prove contagious among mice
      • dubbed the "behavioral sink"
  • later, births stopped and only older mice lingered abandoned in their quarters

Human parallels

  • 19th and 20th centuries saw population booms across the world, largely due to drops in infant mortality
    • similar to the experiment
  • also though human birth rates have dropped sharply in many developed countries—often below replacement levels
  • young people in those places have reportedly lost interest in sex
  • back then behavioral biologists have echoed the eugenics movement in blaming the strange behaviors of the mice on a lack of natural selection
    • lack of culling resulted in supposed “mutational meltdowns” that led to widespread mouse stupidity and aberrant behavior
    • essentially gene mutations caused by the lack of natural selection interfered with brain function because of its inherent sensitivity caused by being heavily defined by those said genes
  • in the modern day, women are supposedly falling into Calhoun’s behavioral sink by learning “maladaptive behaviors”
    • such as choosing not to have children → “destroying their own genetic interests”
  • other critics agonize over the supposed loss of traditional gender roles, leaving effete males and hyperaggressive females
  • it was also viewed at the time as a faulty welfare (socialist) system that removes all challenges from the human life
  • though the fall of Universe 25 might have been caused by other (in human terms solvable) problems
    • above all wealth distribution

Takeaway

Dropping birthrates

  • appearance of the sexless “beautiful ones” does seem decadent and echoes the reported loss of interest in sex among young people in developed countries
    • a closer look at the survey data indicates that such worries might be overblown
    • mouse rates dropped partly due to infant neglect and spikes in infant mortality—the opposite of the situation in the developed world

Dropping intelligence

  • there are the warnings about the mutational meltdown and the decline of intelligence
    • hermit females and preening, asexual males certainly acted oddly but they avoided the fights that beset earlier generations
      • does not appear to prove the "drop in intelligence"
    • some of Calhoun’s research actually saw rodents getting smarter during experiments
  • it was used as a proof for eugenic claims
    • see [[eugenics]]
  • also sometimes technological breakthrough appeared
    • instead of carrying dirt out bit by bit, they packed it all into a ball and rolled it out the tunnel in one trip
      • compared to humankind inventing the wheel
    • by normal rat standards, this was deviant behavior but also a creative breakthrough

Status and isolation

  • males who lost the fights for dominance couldn’t leave to start over elsewhere
    • they were stuck in pathetic, humiliating roles and lacked a meaningful place in society
  • same went for females when they couldn’t nurse or raise pups properly
  • both groups became depressed and angry, and began lashing out
    • because mice are social animals, they need meaningful social roles to feel fulfilled
    • same applies to humans
  • though this interpretation seems like a stretch
    • humans have far more ways of finding meaning in life than pumping out children or dominating some little hierarchy
    • population density among humans doesn’t necessarily correlate with crowding—a feeling of psychological stress
